# Causal Hypotheses

Use this reference when turning causal explanation into hypotheses.

## Theory, Causal Explanation, Hypothesis, Empirical Facts

The chapter links abstract theory, causal explanation, causal hypotheses, and empirical facts.

```text
Theory provides abstract ideas.
Causal explanation states why and how a cause produces an outcome.
Causal hypothesis translates that explanation into a testable statement.
Empirical facts are used to test or revise the hypothesis.
```

## What A Research Hypothesis Is

A research hypothesis is a tentative answer to a research question. It is not the same as an assumption.

- Hypothesis: to be tested.
- Assumption: temporarily accepted premise within a theory or model.

## Requirements For Research Hypotheses

The chapter emphasizes that hypotheses should be:

- related to the research question;
- clear and specific;
- empirically testable;
- internally consistent;
- connected to broader theoretical concerns.

## What Makes A Hypothesis Untestable

A hypothesis is hard to test when:

- it is mainly a value judgment;
- it is too vague;
- it uses circular language;
- it cannot be linked to observable facts;
- it is tautological;
- it does not specify variables or direction.

## Special Requirements For Causal Hypotheses

A causal hypothesis should:

1. Clearly identify the relevant variables.
2. Specify causal direction.
3. Respect temporal order.
4. Suggest a mechanism or be connected to a mechanism.
5. Be open to empirical test.

Template:

```text
If X changes or appears, then Y is more/less likely to occur,
because X produces Y through mechanism M.
```

## Ways To Build Causal Hypotheses

### Empirical induction

```text
Observe facts -> find regularity or contrast -> infer possible cause -> state causal hypothesis
```

### Logical deduction

```text
Theory/assumption -> expected causal relation -> causal hypothesis
```

### Abduction / retroduction

```text
Surprising fact -> possible hidden cause/mechanism -> causal hypothesis -> empirical check
```

## Hypothesis Drafting Checklist

- Does the hypothesis answer the research question?
- Are cause and outcome clear?
- Is causal direction clear?
- Does the cause precede the outcome?
- Is the hypothesis empirically testable?
- Is the statement non-tautological?
- Is the mechanism at least sketched?
- Are the relevant conditions or limits stated if needed?
